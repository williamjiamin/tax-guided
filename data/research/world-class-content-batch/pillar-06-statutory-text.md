## Entry: `26-usc-11`

- Proposed route: `/statutes/us/irc/11`

### Plain-language summary

Section 11 is the modern baseline federal corporate tax rule. It imposes tax on the taxable income of every corporation and, in the current preliminary code text, sets the amount at 21% of taxable income.

### Why it matters

Every corporate-tax explainer in the U.S. stack ultimately sits on top of this section. It is the place where the modern post-TCJA 21% corporate rate becomes positive law.

### Sources

- [26 U.S.C. Section 11](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=%28title%3A26+section%3A11+edition%3Aprelim%29)

## Entry: `26-usc-61`

- Proposed route: `/statutes/us/irc/61`

### Plain-language summary

Section 61 is the gross-income foundation section. It states that, unless another rule says otherwise, gross income means all income from whatever source derived, then lists core categories such as compensation, business income, gains from property, interest, rents, royalties, dividends, annuities, pensions, and partnership gross income.

### Why it matters

This is one of the most important code sections to surface on the site because so many tax disputes begin with the question whether something is included in gross income before the analysis moves to deductions, exclusions, timing, or source.

### Sources

- [26 U.S.C. Section 61](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=%28title%3A26+section%3A61%28a%29+edition%3Aprelim%29)

## Entry: `26-usc-162`

- Proposed route: `/statutes/us/irc/162`

### Plain-language summary

Section 162 is the business-expense anchor. It allows a deduction for ordinary and necessary expenses paid or incurred during the taxable year in carrying on any trade or business, then lists examples such as compensation, travel, and certain rentals.

### Why it matters

This section is the statutory home for one of the most reused phrases in U.S. tax law: "ordinary and necessary." It is where practical expense-deduction analysis begins, and it is central to many founder questions about what a business can deduct.

### Sources

- [26 U.S.C. Section 162](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?edition=2010&num=0&req=granuleid%3AUSC-2010-title26-section162)

## Entry: `26-usc-6038a`

- Proposed route: `/statutes/us/irc/6038a`

### Plain-language summary

Section 6038A is the reporting-and-recordkeeping backbone for certain foreign-owned corporations. The section requires specified reporting corporations to furnish information and maintain records concerning transactions with related parties.

### Why it matters

This is the statutory engine behind the Form 5472 compliance universe. TaxGuided should treat it as a core source page for foreign-owned entity reporting, especially because the practical audience usually sees only the form and not the underlying recordkeeping authority.

### Sources

- [26 U.S.C. Section 6038A](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?edition=prelim&num=0&req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title26-section6038A)

## Entry: `26-usc-864`

- Proposed route: `/statutes/us/irc/864`

### Plain-language summary

Section 864 is one of the definitional centers of cross-border federal income tax. It contains key rules and definitions relating to income from sources within or without the United States and concepts that feed the effectively-connected-income analysis.

### Why it matters

Users asking whether foreign business income is "ECI" often jump straight to conclusion pages. The statute page should remind them that the ECI framework is built from code definitions and then translated into practice by IRS guidance and case law.

### Sources

- [26 U.S.C. Section 864](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?edition=prelim&num=0&req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title26-section864)
- [IRS ECI explainer](https://www.irs.gov/individuals/international-taxpayers/effectively-connected-income-eci)

## Entry: `26-usc-882`

- Proposed route: `/statutes/us/irc/882`

### Plain-language summary

Section 882 is the foreign-corporation charging section for U.S.-business income. It says that a foreign corporation engaged in trade or business within the United States is taxed on taxable income effectively connected with that U.S. trade or business.

### Why it matters

This is the section that turns the ECI concept into actual foreign-corporation tax liability. It belongs on the site because users often understand the words "effectively connected income" but do not know where the tax imposition rule lives.

### Sources

- [26 U.S.C. Section 882](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=%28title%3A26+section%3A882+edition%3Aprelim%29)

## Entry: `26-usc-7701-domestic-foreign`

- Proposed route: `/statutes/us/irc/7701`

### Plain-language summary

Section 7701 is the general definitions section. For TaxGuided's cross-border stack, the most useful pieces are the code definitions of corporation, domestic, and foreign.

### Why it matters

Founders often try to reason about U.S. tax status using business slang rather than statutory definitions. A strong statute page should show that "domestic" and "foreign" are defined legal categories, not casual labels.

### Sources

- [26 U.S.C. Section 7701](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=%28title%3A26+section%3A7701%29)

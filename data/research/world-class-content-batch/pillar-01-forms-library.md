## Entry: `us-irs-5472`

- Proposed route: `/forms/us/5472`
- Content type: form detail
- Authority: IRS
- Current instruction set used: `Instructions for Form 5472 (12/2024)`

### Summary

Form 5472 is the core information return for foreign-owned U.S. corporations and, by regulation, for foreign-owned U.S. disregarded entities that must file it with a pro forma Form 1120. For the TaxGuided audience, this is one of the highest-value form pages on the site because it sits at the center of foreign-founder compliance risk.

### Key facts

- Foreign-owned U.S. disregarded entities cannot electronically file Form 5472.
- A foreign-owned U.S. disregarded entity attaches Form 5472 to a pro forma Form 1120.
- The entity can request more time by filing Form 7004 by the regular due date of the return.
- The base penalty is $25,000 for failure to file when due and in the prescribed manner.
- If the failure continues more than 90 days after IRS notice, an additional $25,000 penalty applies.

### Article copy

The best Form 5472 page should start by removing a common misconception: this is not an income-tax form in the normal sense. It is an information return aimed at related-party reporting. That distinction matters because many founders assume a zero-revenue year means there is nothing to file. For foreign-owned U.S. disregarded entities, that assumption is dangerous. Capital contributions, reimbursements, loans, and other owner-to-entity movements can still create the reporting problem.

The practical filing workflow is also more specific than many summaries suggest. A foreign-owned U.S. disregarded entity files Form 5472 attached to a pro forma Form 1120. The form is not available for electronic filing in that disregarded-entity situation, so the page should make paper or fax filing expectations unmistakable. The site should also cross-link directly to the Form 7004 extension page because the extension only works if it is filed by the regular due date.

This form page should speak plainly about penalty exposure. The IRS instructions say the base penalty is $25,000 for failure to file when due and in the prescribed manner, and that filing a substantially incomplete form is treated as a failure to file. If the failure continues for more than 90 days after IRS notification, another $25,000 penalty can apply. For TaxGuided, this means the page should not be written as a dry catalog entry. It should be written like a high-risk compliance page.

- [About Form 5472](https://www.irs.gov/forms-pubs/about-form-5472)

## Entry: `us-irs-7004`

- Proposed route: `/forms/us/7004`
- Content type: form detail
- Authority: IRS
- Current instruction set used: `Instructions for Form 7004 (12/2025)`

### Summary

Form 7004 is the standard automatic extension request for many business income-tax and information returns. It is a critical companion page because it is how many foreign-owned entities preserve time to file Form 5472 packages correctly.

### Key facts

- Form 7004 generally must be filed on or before the due date of the applicable return.
- It extends time to file, not time to pay.
- The filer must make a proper estimate of tax if tax is due.
- For foreign-owned disregarded entities filing Form 5472 with a pro forma Form 1120, the code for Form 1120 is used on Form 7004.

### Article copy

Form 7004 should be described as a deadline-management form, not as a cure for every late-filing problem. The IRS instructions are explicit that the extension request generally must be filed on or before the due date of the applicable return. Missing that original deadline usually means the extension strategy is already broken.

The page should also correct another frequent mistake: an extension of time to file is not an extension of time to pay. If the taxpayer owes tax, the estimate and payment piece still matters. That distinction is especially important for businesses that are used to thinking about informational filings only. Once a return package mixes information reporting with tax exposure, the extension conversation becomes more than a paperwork issue.

For TaxGuided readers, the page should show the most common use case directly: filing Form 7004 to extend the due date for a Form 5472 package attached to a pro forma Form 1120. This is the right place to give a simple operational warning that the extension works only if filed by the original due date.

### Sources

- [Instructions for Form 7004](https://www.irs.gov/instructions/i7004)
- [About Form 7004](https://www.irs.gov/forms-pubs/about-form-7004)

## Entry: `us-irs-ss-4`

- Proposed route: `/forms/us/ss-4`
- Content type: form detail
- Authority: IRS
- Current instruction set used: `Instructions for Form SS-4 (12/2025)`

### Summary

Form SS-4 is the IRS application for an employer identification number. For foreign founders, the page should be treated as a setup-and-compliance bridge page because the EIN is the identifier that unlocks banking, tax filing, payroll, and many post-formation workflows.

### Key facts

- Form SS-4 is used to apply for an EIN.
- The IRS recommends applying electronically if possible.
- The EIN is the entity's federal tax identification number for reporting and filing.
- This is not an annual form; it is an onboarding form used when the entity needs an EIN.

### Article copy

The usual beginner mistake with Form SS-4 is treating it like a mere administrative box-check. In practice, it is the identification document that makes the rest of the compliance stack possible. Without an EIN, routine next steps such as federal filing, certain banking workflows, and payroll setup can stall quickly.

The page should keep its scope disciplined. It is not the right place to turn into a general entity-formation guide, but it should explain that the IRS recommends electronic application when possible and that the EIN is the federal identifier used for tax filing and reporting. For foreign founders, the most useful version of this page will connect the EIN step to the next actions that matter: bookkeeping setup, federal forms, and any state registrations that require the federal number.

### Sources

- [Instructions for Form SS-4](https://www.irs.gov/instructions/iss4)
- [About Form SS-4](https://www.irs.gov/forms-pubs/about-form-ss-4)

## Entry: `us-ca-ftb-3522`

- Proposed route: `/forms/us/ca/ftb-3522`
- Content type: form detail
- Authority: California Franchise Tax Board

### Summary

FTB 3522 is the California LLC Tax Voucher used for the annual LLC tax payment. This page matters because California is one of the easiest places for foreign founders to underestimate ongoing state-level cost.

### Key facts

- Every LLC doing business in California or organized in California must generally pay the $800 annual tax.
- The annual tax is generally due by the 15th day of the 4th month after the beginning of the taxable year.
- FTB 3522 is the payment voucher tied to that obligation.
- The special first-year exemption applied only for tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2021 and before January 1, 2024.

### Article copy

This page should not talk about California as if the state only becomes relevant once a business is profitable. The FTB's LLC guidance is much stricter than that. The annual tax exists because the LLC is doing business in California or is organized there, and it continues until the entity is properly cancelled.

For a calendar-year LLC in 2026, the annual tax deadline is April 15, 2026. The page should say this plainly, then explain the recurring rule behind it. It should also call out the expired first-year exemption window so readers do not rely on stale posts that still describe the 2021 through 2023 relief as current law.

## Entry: `us-ca-ftb-3536`

- Proposed route: `/forms/us/ca/ftb-3536`
- Content type: form detail
- Authority: California Franchise Tax Board

### Summary

FTB 3536 is the Estimated Fee for LLCs payment form. It belongs on the site because California LLCs can owe more than the fixed $800 annual tax when California-source total income is high enough.

### Key facts

- California separates the annual tax from the LLC fee.
- The estimated LLC fee payment is due by the 15th day of the 6th month of the current tax year.
- The final fee amount is reconciled on the return.
- The fee is tied to total income from California sources.

### Article copy

The main value of an FTB 3536 page is that it teaches readers California has two different LLC cost layers. The $800 annual tax is one layer. The income-based LLC fee is another. A founder who pays only the annual tax and never looks at the fee rules can still end up noncompliant.

For a calendar-year LLC in 2026, the estimated fee deadline is June 15, 2026. The page should explain that the fee is estimated during the year and then reconciled on the return. It should also point users toward the California fee chart for the live thresholds and emphasize that the fee turns on California-source total income, not simply worldwide profit.

### Sources

- [California business due dates](https://www.ftb.ca.gov/file/when-to-file/due-dates-business.html)
- [Web Pay LLC payment types](https://webapp.ftb.ca.gov/WebPay/Help/LLC)

## Entry: `uk-self-assessment-workflow`

- Proposed route: `/forms/uk/self-assessment`
- Content type: filing workflow guide
- Authority: HMRC

### Summary

This is not a single PDF form page. It is the actual HMRC filing workflow page TaxGuided should publish for first-time Self Assessment users, because the official process is spread across registration, filing, deadline, and penalty pages.

### Key facts

- First-time filers who need to send a return for the prior tax year must generally tell HMRC by 5 October after that tax year ends.
- The paper filing deadline is generally 31 October after the end of the tax year.
- The online filing deadline is generally 31 January after the end of the tax year.
- The balance due is generally also due by 31 January.
- Late filing triggers a fixed penalty and escalating later penalties.

### Article copy

The official HMRC materials show why Self Assessment should be treated as a workflow, not a single form. Registration happens first. Filing deadlines depend on whether the return is paper or electronic. Payment timing matters separately. Penalties escalate if the process is ignored.

For the 2024 to 2025 tax year, the registration page says taxpayers who need to complete a return and have not sent one before must tell HMRC by October 5, 2025. HMRC's legal framework material then anchors the standard filing dates: October 31 for paper returns and January 31 for electronic returns, with January 31 also serving as the general payment due date for the related tax.

The penalty page makes the risk concrete. Late filing starts with a GBP 100 penalty, then daily penalties after three months, then further charges after six and twelve months. A strong TaxGuided page should combine those scattered official rules into one calm but practical timeline.

- [HMRC legal framework for Self Assessment](https://www.gov.uk/hmrc-internal-manuals/self-assessment-legal-framework/salf202)

## Entry: `uk-us-individual-2002`

- Proposed route: `/forms/uk/us-individual-2002`
- Content type: treaty relief form guide
- Authority: HMRC

### Summary

Form US-Individual 2002 is the operational route for many U.S. residents claiming treaty relief from UK tax on certain UK-source income categories. This is the right page to connect treaty theory with actual HMRC procedure.

### Key facts

- HMRC uses form US-Individual 2002 for U.S. individuals claiming relief at source or repayment under the UK-U.S. treaty for qualifying income.
- HMRC's HS304 helpsheet explains the underlying non-resident treaty relief framework.
- HS304 says U.S. residents do not need a certificate of overseas residence because the U.S. taxes residents on worldwide income.
- Claimants still need to match the income type, treaty article, and supporting evidence.

### Article copy

A good treaty-relief page should begin by correcting the lazy version of treaty advice. The existence of a treaty does not make reduced withholding appear automatically. HMRC expects a process, and that process depends on the kind of UK-source income involved.

HS304 explains the broader non-resident relief framework and lists income types that may qualify, including interest, royalties, many pensions, annuities, and UK dividends. The form-specific UK-U.S. materials then turn that legal idea into a filing path. For U.S. residents, one especially useful operational point is that HMRC says a certificate of overseas residence is not required in the same way it is for many other countries because the U.S. taxes its residents on worldwide income.

The real value of the TaxGuided page is to help users build a treaty file before money starts moving: identify the income category, confirm the relevant treaty position, gather evidence, and use the correct HMRC claim route.

## Entry: `us-uk`

- Proposed route: `/treaties/us-uk`
- Treaty family: income tax treaty

### Summary

The U.S.-UK treaty should be one of the first full treaty pages built on the site because it has high founder relevance, strong cross-border investment volume, and clean official source material from both the IRS and the Treasury/HMRC side.

### Core themes to highlight

- Business profits analysis depends heavily on permanent-establishment questions.
- U.S. and UK founders regularly encounter treaty issues around dividends, interest, royalties, pensions, and dual-residency questions.
- UK-side relief claims often require a form workflow rather than abstract treaty awareness.
- The treaty should be paired with a practical page on UK form `US-Individual 2002`.

### Article copy

The U.S.-UK treaty page should not try to be a generic "double tax is bad" explainer. It should start where users actually get stuck: residence, permanent establishment, income category, and claim mechanics. For a founder or investor, the treaty becomes real when one country taxes the income at source and the other country still sees it as part of the residence-country return.

The UK-side guidance on non-resident treaty relief is useful because it forces evidence discipline. Claimants need to identify the income stream, confirm the treaty route, and organize proof that they meet the treaty conditions. The treaty page should therefore have two layers: a legal layer keyed to the official treaty text and a workflow layer keyed to real claim steps.

## Entry: `us-canada`

- Proposed route: `/treaties/us-canada`
- Treaty family: income tax treaty

### Summary

The U.S.-Canada treaty deserves early expansion because it is one of the most-used North American treaty frameworks and has unusually practical official support material in IRS Publication 597.

### Core themes to highlight

- Publication 597 gives unusually practical treatment of articles affecting self-employment, services PE, pensions, retirement plans, and investment income.
- Cross-border individuals often care about pensions, social security, dividends, interest, royalties, and foreign tax credit interactions.
- The treaty page should not be limited to withholding tables; it should explain residency and article selection.

### Article copy

A strong U.S.-Canada treaty page should use the treaty text and Publication 597 together. The treaty itself supplies the legal authority. Publication 597 adds the practical bridge that ordinary users actually need. That makes this treaty one of the best candidates for a page that combines article-by-article navigation with scenario-specific summaries.

The page should foreground the areas the IRS publication itself emphasizes: self-employment, services permanent establishment, pensions and retirement arrangements, and common types of Canadian-source investment income. Founders who move between the two countries often care less about a complete legal digest than about whether the treaty changes withholding, creates reporting differences, or interacts with their foreign tax credit position.

### Sources

- [IRS Publication 597](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p597)
- [Official U.S.-Canada treaty PDF](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-trty/canada.pdf)
- [IRS Publication 901](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p901)

## Entry: `us-germany`

- Proposed route: `/treaties/us-germany`
- Treaty family: income tax treaty

### Summary

The U.S.-Germany treaty should be treated as a high-value founder and investor page because Germany regularly appears in cross-border holding, royalty, employment, and permanent-establishment questions.

### Core themes to highlight

- Business profits and permanent-establishment issues are central.
- The treaty should be read together with the 2006 protocol and later technical explanation material.
- A treaty page should explain that the withholding result depends on the relevant article and on whether the recipient meets treaty conditions.

### Article copy

The U.S.-Germany page should avoid a shallow "Germany has a treaty with the U.S." framing. The treaty matters because it can change the tax outcome for business profits, employment income, royalties, dividends, and other recurring cross-border categories. But it only does that through specific articles applied to specific facts.

The official IRS treaty documents page is especially useful here because it clearly identifies the 1989 treaty materials as well as the later protocol and technical explanation. TaxGuided should use that structure directly: treaty text first, protocol changes second, practical article navigation third.

- [Official U.S.-Germany treaty PDF](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-trty/germany.pdf)
- [Technical explanation / protocol material](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-trty/germanyte07.pdf)

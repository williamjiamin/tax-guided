## Entry: `us-ca`

- Proposed route: `/states/us-ca`
- Internal seed ID already in repo: `us-ca`

### Summary

California should be treated as the "high-cost, high-exposure" state guide in the expansion program. It is one of the most important state pages because it combines annual entity costs, separate return rules, and sales-tax complexity.

### Key facts

- Every LLC doing business in California or organized in California generally owes an $800 annual tax.
- The annual tax is generally due on the 15th day of the 4th month after the beginning of the taxable year.
- California can also impose an LLC fee based on total income from California sources.
- For calendar-year single-member LLCs owned by an individual or non-pass-through entity, the return due date is generally the 15th day of the 4th month after year end.
- California's statewide sales-tax rate is 7.25%, with district taxes that can push the combined rate higher.

### Article copy

California is where the "I only formed the LLC, I have not really started yet" mindset breaks down quickly. The FTB's LLC page makes clear that the annual tax applies because the entity is doing business in California or is organized there. It is not a profit test.

The state guide should also separate the annual tax from the annual fee. These are not the same charge. The annual tax is the fixed baseline payment. The annual fee is income-based and tied to California-source total income. A serious state guide has to explain both layers clearly, because founders often discover only one of them.

The sales-tax angle matters too. California's statewide rate starts at 7.25%, but district taxes can increase the combined rate. Marketplace facilitator rules reduce some seller friction, but they do not eliminate the need to understand California-source sales and the right registration posture.

## Entry: `us-tx`

- Proposed route: `/states/us-tx`
- Internal seed ID already in repo: `us-tx`

### Summary

Texas deserves a prominent state guide because founders often hear "no state income tax" and stop thinking. The correct guide is about franchise tax, information reports, and marketplace or nexus consequences.

### Key facts

- Texas does not impose a state individual income tax.
- Texas franchise tax rates and thresholds vary by report year.
- For 2026 and 2027, the official no-tax-due threshold is $2,650,000.
- For 2026 and 2027, the tax rate is 0.375% for retail or wholesale entities and 0.75% for other taxable entities.
- For reports due on or after January 1, 2024, entities at or below the threshold no longer file a No Tax Due Report, but they still generally file a Public Information Report or Ownership Information Report.

### Article copy

Texas is one of the best examples of why a state page should not chase slogans. "No income tax" is true for individuals, but it does not mean "no annual state compliance." The Texas system revolves around franchise tax and related reporting.

The official franchise tax page gives TaxGuided a strong historical table and a current-law anchor. For report years 2026 and 2027, the no-tax-due threshold is $2.65 million, with the retail or wholesale rate at 0.375% and the general rate at 0.75%. The 2026 forms page also clarifies a rule that many older guides miss: a below-threshold entity generally no longer files the No Tax Due Report, but it still files the PIR or OIR.

The sales-tax side also matters for e-commerce. Texas says marketplace sellers remain responsible for understanding whether the marketplace provider has certified that it is collecting on their behalf, and Texas sellers may still need permits and timely returns depending on the facts.

## Entry: `us-de`

- Proposed route: `/states/us-de`
- Content type: state guide

### Summary

Delaware is one of the most common U.S. formation states for foreign founders, so its state guide should be brutally clear about the one rule people forget: LLCs do not file an annual report, but they still owe the annual tax.

### Key facts

- Domestic and foreign Delaware LLCs generally owe a $300 annual tax.
- The tax is due on or before June 1 each year.
- Delaware LLCs do not file an annual report.
- The penalty for non-payment is $200.
- Interest accrues at 1.5% per month on the tax and penalty.

### Article copy

Delaware's practical value comes from legal familiarity and founder recognition, but the maintenance rule is simpler and harsher than many users expect. The state does not ask an LLC for an annual report, which misleads some owners into believing the entity has no annual state filing burden at all. That is wrong. The annual tax still has to be paid.

The Delaware page should be designed to kill that misunderstanding fast. A foreign founder choosing Delaware for formation convenience still needs a system for the June 1 annual tax deadline. If the tax is not paid on time, Delaware adds a $200 penalty and monthly interest at 1.5% on both tax and penalty. That makes this a perfect example of a page that can be short but high-value.

### Sources

- [Delaware annual report and tax information](https://corp.delaware.gov/frtax/)
- [Delaware annual report and tax instructions](https://corp.delaware.gov/paytaxes/)
- [Delaware tax FAQ](https://corp.delaware.gov/taxfaq/)

## Entry: `us-wy`

- Proposed route: `/states/us-wy`
- Content type: state guide

### Summary

Wyoming is the low-cost benchmark state guide. It is important not because it is complicated, but because its simplicity makes it one of the most common formation states for small foreign-owned LLCs.

### Key facts

- Wyoming requires an annual report beginning with the first anniversary cycle.
- The report is due on or before the first day of the month of the entity's initial filing date.
- Wyoming official materials describe a minimum annual report fee of $60.
- The Secretary of State sends reminder notices by email.

### Article copy

The Wyoming guide should be written like an operational checklist. Founders choose Wyoming because the annual burden is comparatively light, but that does not mean there is no annual burden. The state says the annual report is due on or before the first day of the month of the initial filing date, beginning with the first anniversary year.

That deadline rule is easy to miss because it is not a universal calendar date like May 1 or June 1. It is anniversary-based. TaxGuided should therefore present the rule with an example, such as a February 16 formation producing a February 1 annual-report due date in the next year.

## Entry: `us-fl`

- Proposed route: `/states/us-fl`
- Content type: state guide

### Summary

Florida's guide should sit at the intersection of formation maintenance and tax exposure. It is especially useful for founders who assume that "no personal state income tax" means "minimal state compliance."

### Key facts

- Florida LLC annual reports are due by May 1 each year.
- The on-time LLC annual report fee is $138.75.
- A $400 late fee applies after May 1 for LLCs that want to remain active.
- Failure to file by the 3rd Friday of September results in administrative dissolution or revocation at the close of business on the 4th Friday of September.
- Florida's general state sales-tax rate is 6%.
- Florida corporate income/franchise tax is 5.5% for taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2022.

### Article copy

Florida is attractive because of the no-personal-income-tax headline, but the correct operational guide focuses on the annual report and on whether the entity is actually in the Florida corporate-tax bucket. For an LLC, the annual report is the state-maintenance issue that matters every year.

The Sunbiz materials are specific enough that the page should speak in exact dates and amounts. The annual report is due by May 1. The on-time fee for an LLC is $138.75. After May 1, a $400 late fee applies. If the entity still does not file, Florida moves toward administrative dissolution in September.

For C corporations and entities taxed federally as corporations, Florida also has a corporate income/franchise tax system. The Department of Revenue page says the rate is 5.5% for taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2022. The state guide should therefore distinguish clearly between entity maintenance, pass-through treatment, and corporate-tax exposure.

## Entry: `ecommerce-marketplace-facilitator-california`

- Proposed route: `/industry/ecommerce/california-marketplace-facilitator-guide`
- Industry: e-commerce

### Summary

California should be one of the first e-commerce industry guides because the CDTFA marketplace-facilitator materials are clear, official, and commercially important.

### Article copy

The California e-commerce guide should begin with the rule that changed the operating map for many sellers: beginning October 1, 2019, a marketplace facilitator is generally treated as the retailer responsible for collecting, reporting, and paying the tax on marketplace sales delivered to California customers. That fact alone saves sellers from a lot of bad internet advice.

But the guide should not oversell the relief. Marketplace-facilitator treatment only solves the marketplace piece. Sellers still need to understand their direct-sales channel, their records, and whether they have separate sales or fee obligations outside the facilitated transactions. This is where a strong industry page can beat generic blog content: it explains what the facilitator handles, what the seller still needs to document, and why recordkeeping remains essential.

### Sources

- [California Marketplace Facilitator Act guide](https://www.cdtfa.ca.gov/industry/MPFAct.htm)

## Entry: `ecommerce-marketplace-seller-texas`

- Proposed route: `/industry/ecommerce/texas-marketplace-seller-guide`
- Industry: e-commerce

### Summary

Texas is a useful companion guide because it shows the distinction between marketplace sales-tax collection and separate franchise-tax or permit obligations.

### Article copy

The Texas marketplace guide should lead with the split the Comptroller makes explicit. If a marketplace provider certifies that it is collecting and remitting sales tax on the seller's behalf, the seller generally does not collect tax on those marketplace transactions itself. But that does not erase every Texas obligation.

Texas says that a Texas seller may still need a Texas sales-tax permit and timely returns even if its only sales are through a marketplace provider. Texas also says that a marketplace seller that is a taxable entity may still have franchise-tax obligations if it has physical presence or economic nexus in Texas. That makes this guide especially valuable for foreign-owned LLCs that confuse marketplace collection relief with total state-tax immunity.

### Sources

- [Texas marketplace providers and marketplace sellers](https://comptroller.texas.gov/taxes/sales/marketplace-providers-sellers.php)
- [Texas franchise tax page](https://comptroller.texas.gov/taxes/franchise/)

## Entry: `crypto-us-digital-assets`

- Proposed route: `/industry/crypto/us-digital-assets-guide`
- Industry: crypto and digital assets

### Summary

The IRS digital-assets page should anchor a practical U.S. crypto guide because it reflects the government's own framing of reporting and recordkeeping.

### Article copy

The crypto guide should not begin with exotic token edge cases. It should begin with the IRS framing: digital assets are a reporting category the Service expects taxpayers to address directly, and ordinary tax rules still apply to transactions involving them.

The most useful TaxGuided version of this page would explain that crypto tax questions are often not special because crypto is magical; they are special because users do not keep transaction records at the level ordinary tax computation requires. The page should therefore combine classification, transaction examples, and documentation habits in one place.

### Sources

- [IRS digital assets page](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/digital-assets)

## Entry: `real-estate-firpta-guide`

- Proposed route: `/industry/real-estate/firpta-guide`
- Industry: real estate

### Summary

FIRPTA should be one of the first real-estate guides because foreign owners consistently underestimate how withholding works when U.S. real property changes hands.

### Article copy

The real-estate guide should begin with a plain operational truth: FIRPTA is a withholding system, not just a substantive tax rule. Foreign sellers often focus on the final gain calculation and miss the cash-flow effect at closing. That is the wrong sequence. The withholding question hits first.

The guide should also separate property-sale withholding from partnership or entity-level issues. A multi-member LLC taxed as a partnership adds a second layer of analysis that many users never see coming. TaxGuided can win here by publishing a clean owner-facing guide that explains when the buyer withholds, when the entity may also have withholding responsibilities, and why early planning matters if a withholding certificate will be needed.

### Sources

- [IRS FIRPTA overview](https://www.irs.gov/individuals/international-taxpayers/firpta)
- [IRS FIRPTA withholding](https://www.irs.gov/individuals/international-taxpayers/firpta-withholding)
- [IRS definitions and procedures unique to FIRPTA](https://www.irs.gov/individuals/international-taxpayers/definitions-of-terms-and-procedures-unique-to-firpta)

## Entry: `uae-consultants-and-solo-operators`

- Proposed route: `/industry/consulting/uae-natural-person-corporate-tax-guide`
- Industry: consulting and solo services

### Summary

This is one of the best niche guides for the UAE because many solo operators still rely on outdated "tax-free" simplifications.

### Article copy

The UAE consulting guide should be written around the natural-person threshold rule, not around old marketing slogans. The Federal Tax Authority says a natural person is subject to Corporate Tax only if the person conducts business or business activity in the UAE and total turnover from that activity exceeds AED 1 million in the calendar year.

The guide should emphasize that wages, personal investment income, and real-estate investment income are not treated the same way as business turnover for this purpose. That distinction matters because solo consultants often mix all cash inflows together and then either overstate or understate their corporate-tax exposure.

### Sources

- [FTA basis of taxation for natural persons](https://tax.gov.ae/en/taxes/corporate.tax/corporate.tax.topics/basis.of.taxation.natural.person.aspx)
- [UAE Ministry of Finance corporate tax page](https://mof.gov.ae/en/public-finance/tax/corporate-tax-in-the-uae/)

## Entry: `hospitality-switzerland-accommodation-vat`

- Proposed route: `/industry/hospitality/switzerland-accommodation-vat-guide`
- Industry: hospitality

### Summary

This is a high-quality niche guide because the Swiss VAT rate structure creates an immediate industry-specific distinction for hotels and accommodation businesses.

### Article copy

The Swiss hospitality guide should begin with the special rate itself. The Federal Tax Administration says accommodation services, including overnight stays with breakfast in the hotel and accommodation business, are subject to a special 3.8% VAT rate. That single rule makes a generic Swiss VAT page insufficient for this industry.

The page should then explain the operational trap: not every supply made by a hotel or accommodation business automatically falls into the accommodation rate bucket. Room revenue and mixed services need careful classification. A strong guide can help businesses separate their accommodation supplies from goods or other services that may fall into different VAT categories.

### Sources

- [Swiss VAT rates](https://www.estv.admin.ch/en/vat-rates-switzerland)

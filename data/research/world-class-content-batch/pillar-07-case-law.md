## Entry: `moline-properties-v-commissioner`

- Proposed route: `/case-law/us/supreme-court/1943/moline-properties-v-commissioner`
- Citation: `319 U.S. 436 (1943)`

### Holding in plain English

The Supreme Court held that a corporation's gains from selling its property could not simply be treated as the sole shareholder's income instead. If the corporation exists for a business purpose or actually carries on business activity, the corporate form is respected for federal income-tax purposes.

### Why it matters

This is a cornerstone case for founder audiences because it pushes back on the casual idea that a wholly owned company can be ignored whenever doing so would be tax-efficient. It is one of the cleanest judicial anchors for entity separateness.

## Entry: `higgins-v-commissioner`

- Proposed route: `/case-law/us/supreme-court/1941/higgins-v-commissioner`
- Citation: `312 U.S. 212 (1941)`

### Holding in plain English

The Supreme Court held that salaries and other expenses incurred in looking after one's own stock and bond investments were not deductible as expenses of carrying on a trade or business.

### Why it matters

This case is central whenever users ask whether active management of their own investments turns them into a "trade or business." The answer is not automatically yes, even if the activity is large, organized, and time-consuming.

## Entry: `commissioner-v-groetzinger`

- Proposed route: `/case-law/us/supreme-court/1987/commissioner-v-groetzinger`
- Citation: `480 U.S. 23 (1987)`

### Holding in plain English

The Supreme Court held that a taxpayer who devoted full time to parimutuel wagering, with continuity and regularity and for the production of income for a livelihood, was engaged in a trade or business.

### Why it matters

This is one of the most cited modern "trade or business" cases because it shows the test is intensely factual. It is useful to pair this case with `Higgins` so users can see that investment management and business activity are not the same category.

### Source

- [Official opinion PDF](https://tile.loc.gov/storage-services/service/ll/usrep/usrep480/usrep480023/usrep480023.pdf)

## Entry: `deputy-v-du-pont`

- Proposed route: `/case-law/us/supreme-court/1940/deputy-v-du-pont`
- Citation: `308 U.S. 488 (1940)`

### Holding in plain English

The Supreme Court held that the taxpayer could not deduct certain expenditures as "ordinary and necessary" business expenses because they were not ordinary in the relevant business and did not properly arise from the taxpayer's own business.

### Why it matters

This case is important because it sharpens the meaning of "ordinary." An expense can feel commercially sensible and still fail the statutory business-expense test.

## Entry: `cottage-savings-v-commissioner`

- Proposed route: `/case-law/us/supreme-court/1991/cottage-savings-v-commissioner`
- Citation: `499 U.S. 554 (1991)`

### Holding in plain English

The Supreme Court held that an exchange of property realizes a tax-deductible loss when the properties exchanged are materially different. In the case, the exchanged mortgage participation interests were materially different enough to trigger realization.

### Why it matters

This is a core realization case. It is useful whenever TaxGuided explains why tax consequences often turn on a disposition or exchange event rather than on annual market fluctuations alone.
